TP5410 Synchronous Buck Converter

3A Output Current • 85% Efficiency • 500kHz Switching Frequency

Product Overview

The TP5410 is a high-efficiency synchronous step-down (buck) DC-DC converter that integrates two 30mΩ power MOSFETs and supports up to 3A continuous output current from a 2.5V to 5.5V input supply. The device operates at a fixed 500kHz switching frequency to allow for small external components and good efficiency across the entire load range.

The TP5410 offers a wide output voltage range from 0.6V to VIN with an adjustable version, and a fixed output voltage version for simpler designs. Current mode control provides fast line and load transient response with excellent loop stability. Protection features include thermal shutdown, soft-start, cycle-by-cycle current limiting, and over-voltage protection.

Key Features

  • Input Voltage Range: 2.5V to 5.5V

Field Application Engineer Insights

When designing with the TP5410, select external components carefully to optimize performance. Use low ESR ceramic output capacitors for good transient response. For the inductor, choose one rated for the saturation current at the maximum output current.

The small package (TSOT-23-6) requires careful PCB layout to ensure proper thermal management. Place the input capacitor as close to the IC as possible to minimize parasitic inductance. The current mode control provides good stability but requires proper compensation if adjusting the output voltage.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between synchronous and asynchronous buck converters?

Synchronous buck converters like the TP5410 use two active switches (MOSFETs) on both high-side and low-side, improving efficiency by replacing the low-side diode with an active switch. Asynchronous converters use a diode on the low-side. Synchronous converters are more efficient, especially at high load currents, but slightly more complex.

How do I calculate the power dissipation of the TP5410?

Power dissipation consists of conduction losses and switching losses. For conduction losses: Pcond = IOUT² × (RDSON_HS × D + RDSON_LS × (1-D)), where D is the duty cycle. Switching losses increase with frequency. Total power dissipation determines junction temperature rise above ambient.

Can I use the TP5410 with a 1.8V input?

No, the TP5410 has a minimum input voltage of 2.5V. Operating below this voltage may cause improper regulation or damage to the device. For applications with 1.8V input, consider using the step-up/step-down capability of a SEPIC converter or a different converter solution.
